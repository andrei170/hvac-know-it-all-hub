Section One. The hook (0:00 to 0:45)

Visual: Gary at the home studio or the truck cab. Mid-shot.

Voice (in Gary's operator-grounded register):

"Twenty years on the tools. Refrigeration ticket, Gas 1 ticket, still installing condensers at Combined Air every week.

If you watch the HVAC Know It All channel, you already know the format. Long-form podcast, real techs, real shops, real failures. No flash.

Five minutes. I am going to walk you through who the Business Track is for, what the work is, and how to know if it fits where you are right now. If it does not fit, the next five minutes saves you a click. If it does, the application is one tap underneath this video."

Section Two. The problem (0:45 to 1:45)

Visual: Cut to a simple typographic card listing the four most common shop-owner-track stages. Back to Gary.

Voice:

"Here is the gap I keep hearing on the podcast.

Senior techs ready to launch the first shop. No business owner training in the trade schools. The provincial colleges teach the ticket. The manufacturer programs teach the equipment. Nobody walks the licensing audit, the pricing structure, the first five service-call leads, the basic CRM, the hire-the-first-tech checklist, or the first-ninety-days cash plan.

Shop owners running one to five techs. The owner is still on the truck because nobody mapped the route, the productised service agreement, the hiring scaffolding, or the owner-off-tools transition. The ceiling is real and it sits on the owner.

The technical training is everywhere. The business-owner training for the trades is a desert.

That is the gap. That is what the Business Track fills."

Section Three. The mechanism (1:45 to 3:15)

Visual: Cut to a two-column card showing Track A and Track B modules. Back to Gary.

Voice:

"Two tracks. Same operator over twelve months.

Track A. Launch Smarter. Eight weeks. For the senior tech ready to start the shop or the first-year shop owner.

Week one is the licensing audit for your province or state. Week two is the pricing structure that survives a slow week. Week three is the first five service-call leads and how to get them. Week four is the basic CRM and the scheduling. Week five is the hire-the-first-tech checklist. Week six is the first-ninety-days cash plan. Week seven is the operator review with the cohort. Week eight is the graduation and the bridge to Track B if you are ready.

Track B. Scale Smarter. Six weeks. For the one-to-five-tech shop owner ready to break the owner-on-tools ceiling.

Week one is the route optimisation. Week two is the hiring and retention plan for the 2026 labour market. Week three is the productised service agreement and recurring revenue. Week four is the local-service ad basics on Meta and Google. Week five is the owner-off-tools transition plan. Week six is the operator review and graduation.

Both tracks cap at twenty-five to thirty operators per cycle. Both run quarterly. The room is small on purpose. The Tuesday evening call plus the Friday office-hours block plus the cohort channel are the cadence. The work between calls is three to six hours."

Section Four. The stakes (3:15 to 4:30)

Visual: Card with three bullet points framing the cost of staying where you are. Back to Gary.

Voice:

"Here is what staying where you are looks like.

If you are the senior tech ready to launch, every year you stay in someone else's shop is a year of building someone else's book of business. The work you do builds their pipeline, not yours. The customer relationships you build belong to the truck you do not own.

If you are the one-to-five-tech shop owner stuck on the truck, every install you run is an install your bench tech could have run. Every quote you write at the kitchen table at ten at night is a quote a CSR could have written at three in the afternoon. The ceiling is not the market. The ceiling is the owner being the bottleneck.

Nothing on this video is a promise of a revenue number. Anybody selling you a guaranteed first-year shop revenue in the trades is selling you something else. The Business Track sells operator development. The numbers you produce are yours.

What I can promise is the work, the room, and the cadence. The rest is on the operator."

Section Five. The offer (4:30 to 5:30)

Visual: Card listing what is in the Business Track. Back to Gary.

Voice:

"What you get when you are in.

Eight weeks of Track A or six weeks of Track B, whichever your scorecard band routes you into. Tuesday evening cohort call. Friday office-hours block. The cohort channel open seven days a week. The full curriculum walk module by module. The written deliverables out of every module - the pricing structure, the cash plan, the hire checklist, the route plan, whatever the track produces.

Twenty-five to thirty operators in the room. The room is sized to keep the discussion real.

Application gate. Four-minute Typeform. Manual review within forty-eight hours. If the band is right and the timing fits, you get the enrolment link direct. If it does not fit, you get an honest route to the right next step - the free content, the app, the Facebook community, or a peer-network room if you are past the cohort fit.

Quarterly cycles. The application stays open between cycles and routes to the next cycle automatically.

The exact pricing per cycle surfaces on the application. The tier is mid-four-figure. The tier is not entry-level. The room is sized for the operator who is ready to do the work."

Register guardrails (mandatory across the cut)

  • No lifestyle B-roll. No flashy field cuts. No motivational music sting. Gary's existing register is technician-to-technician, plain English, time-on-tools-respected. The VSL sits inside that register.
  • No income claim. No "make your first $100K in HVAC," no "six-figure HVAC business," no "make $X by month X." The disclaimer at the end is explicit on this.
  • No echo of any other HVAC creator's signature phrasing. The script does not borrow framing from AC Service Tech, HVAC School, or TruTechTools. The script is in Gary's voice or it is rewritten.
  • No claim of certification authority. The Business Track is operator development. Ticketed trade training stays with the provincial colleges and the manufacturer-certified programs. The cohort marketing acknowledges this.
  • Operator-grounded only. "I have been on the tools twenty years" is the authority anchor. "Still installing condensers at Combined Air every week" is the credibility wall. No guru framing, no "transform your business," no "level up your shop."
  • Single VSL variant. English only. Captions burned in.

What this VSL does not do

  • It does not name a specific revenue outcome for the participant
  • It does not promise a hire rate, a close rate, or a business-growth percentage
  • It does not echo any specific dollar-to-dollar promise from any other HVAC educator
  • It does not market against the provincial colleges or the manufacturer-certified programs
  • It does not show the exact Business Track price on screen (the price surfaces on the application)
  • It does not invoke any motivational-guru framing
  • It does not show Gary in non-operator settings (no lifestyle shots, no travel B-roll, no luxury cuts)
  • It does not run music underneath the voice

  • The disclaimer is mandatory and final.
